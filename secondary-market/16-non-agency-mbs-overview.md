## Introduction

Non-agency mortgage-backed securities (also called **private-label MBS** or **PLS**) are residential mortgage-backed securities issued by private entities — investment banks, commercial banks, and specialty lenders — rather than by government-sponsored enterprises (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) or government agencies (Ginnie Mae). Because they lack an explicit or implicit government guarantee, non-agency MBS carry credit risk that must be managed through structural credit enhancement and evaluated by rating agencies.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Private Label MBS Definition

Private-label MBS are securitizations of residential mortgage loans that do not conform to agency guidelines. The issuer — typically a Wall Street dealer or large bank — pools mortgages into a trust, which issues bonds to investors. Key distinguishing features:

- **No government guarantee**: Investors bear the credit risk of the underlying borrowers.
- **Loan characteristics**: Loans may exceed conforming limits, have non-standard documentation, lower credit scores, or other features that make them ineligible for agency securitization.
- **Structural protection**: Credit risk is managed through tranching and credit enhancement rather than a guarantee.
- **Shelf registrations**: Issuers file shelf registration statements with the SEC (e.g., Form S-3) allowing them to issue multiple deals under a single program over time.

### 2. Historical Evolution

#### Pre-2008 Era (Growth Phase)

| Period | Characteristics |
|--------|----------------|
| **1980s–1990s** | Market emergence; primarily prime jumbo loans exceeding conforming limits; relatively conservative underwriting |
| **2000–2003** | Rapid growth fueled by low interest rates and housing appreciation; expansion into alt-A and subprime segments |
| **2004–2007** | Peak issuance exceeding $1 trillion annually; aggressive underwriting (stated income, interest-only, option ARMs, high CLTV); heavy subprime issuance; rating agency competition drove loose standards |

At its peak in 2006, non-agency issuance reached approximately **$1.2 trillion**, representing nearly 40% of total MBS issuance.

#### The Financial Crisis (2007–2009)

- **Trigger**: Housing prices declined nationally for the first time since the Great Depression, exposing the fragility of non-agency structures.
- **Massive losses**: Subprime and alt-A deals experienced cumulative losses of 20–40%+. Even senior AAA-rated tranches suffered downgrades and losses.
- **Rating agency failures**: Agencies were criticized for conflicts of interest (issuer-pays model), inadequate models, and failure to stress-test for national home price declines.
- **Market shutdown**: New issuance effectively ceased in 2008 and remained dormant for years.
- **Regulatory response**: Dodd-Frank Act (2010), Ability-to-Repay (ATR) rule, Qualified Mortgage (QM) definition, risk retention requirements (5% "skin in the game").

#### Post-Crisis Revival (2010–Present)

- **Slow return**: Market reopened gradually starting around 2012–2013 with prime jumbo deals backed by high-quality collateral.
- **New collateral types**: Re-performing loans (RPL), non-performing loans (NPL), non-QM loans, investor property loans, and credit risk transfer (CRT) deals emerged.
- **Structural improvements**: Stronger credit enhancement, tighter representations and warranties, independent third-party due diligence, loan-level data disclosure.
- **Smaller scale**: Annual issuance has remained well below pre-crisis levels, typically $50–150 billion.

### 3. Types of Non-Agency MBS

#### Prime Jumbo

- **Collateral**: High-quality borrowers (700+ FICO) with loans exceeding conforming limits (currently $766,550 in most areas).
- **Characteristics**: Full documentation, low LTV (typically <80%), owner-occupied properties.
- **Performance**: Historically very low default rates; strongest sector within non-agency.
- **Post-crisis**: First non-agency sector to revive; issuers include JP Morgan, Redwood Trust, and others.

#### Alt-A (Alternative-A)

- **Collateral**: Borrowers with generally good credit but non-standard documentation or loan features.
- **Characteristics**: Stated income/stated asset (SISA), interest-only periods, investor properties, higher LTVs than prime.
- **Pre-crisis**: Massive volume; experienced severe losses (15–30% cumulative).
- **Post-crisis**: Category largely eliminated; non-QM has absorbed some of this space but with stronger underwriting.

#### Subprime

- **Collateral**: Borrowers with impaired credit (FICO typically below 620), high DTI ratios, limited documentation.
- **Characteristics**: Higher coupon rates to compensate for risk, prepayment penalties common, 2/28 and 3/27 ARM structures.
- **Pre-crisis**: Issuance peaked at ~$600 billion in 2006; experienced catastrophic losses (30–50%+).
- **Post-crisis**: Term is largely abandoned; similar borrowers may now be served through non-QM programs with better underwriting.

#### Re-Performing Loans (RPL)

- **Collateral**: Previously delinquent loans that have been modified and are now current.
- **Characteristics**: Often acquired at a discount from banks or GSEs; borrowers have demonstrated re-payment ability post-modification; typically carry lower modified interest rates.
- **Market**: Significant post-crisis volume; GSEs sold large RPL pools to private investors for securitization.
- **Data challenges**: Complex payment histories, modification terms, and re-default risk modeling.

#### Non-Performing Loans (NPL)

- **Collateral**: Loans currently in default or foreclosure.
- **Characteristics**: Purchased at deep discounts; securitization proceeds fund loss mitigation activities; servicer plays a critical role.
- **Resolution strategies**: Loan modification, short sale, deed-in-lieu, REO liquidation.

#### Non-QM (Non-Qualified Mortgage)

- **Collateral**: Loans that do not meet the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) QM definition but are originated with ATR compliance.
- **Common types**: Bank statement loans (self-employed), DSCR investor loans, interest-only, asset depletion, foreign national.
- **Growth**: Fastest-growing non-agency sector since ~2016; issuance has reached $25–40 billion annually.
- **Key issuers**: Angel Oak, A&D Mortgage, Verus Securitization, New Residential.

### 4. Shelf Registrations and Issuance Process

The issuance process for non-agency MBS involves several steps:

1. **Shelf registration**: Issuer files a base prospectus with the SEC under Regulation AB (or AB II post-crisis), allowing multiple offerings.
2. **Prospectus supplement**: For each deal, a supplement is filed with specific pool and structural details.
3. **Due diligence**: Third-party review firms (Clayton, AMC, Opus) review a sample of loans for compliance and quality.
4. **Rating**: One or more rating agencies (S&P, Moody's, Fitch, DBRS Morningstar, Kroll/KBRA) assign ratings to each tranche.
5. **Pricing and settlement**: Bonds are priced (typically as a spread to benchmarks) and settle in the secondary market.

### 5. Rating Agencies Role

Rating agencies evaluate non-agency MBS tranches and assign credit ratings:

| Agency | Key Models/Approach |
|--------|-------------------|
| **S&P Global** | LEVELS model for loss estimation; cash flow modeling for structure |
| **Moody's** | MILAN model for expected loss; Aaa loss benchmark |
| **Fitch** | ResiGlobal model; sustainable home price analysis |
| **DBRS Morningstar** | RMBS Insight model |
| **KBRA (Kroll)** | KBRA Residential Mortgage Loss Model |

**Key rating factors:**
- **Collateral quality**: FICO, LTV, DTI, documentation type, occupancy, property type, geographic concentration
- **Structural features**: Credit enhancement levels, waterfall mechanics, triggers
- **Originator/servicer quality**: Track record, operational capabilities, alignment of interests
- **Macroeconomic assumptions**: Home price scenarios, interest rate paths, unemployment projections

**Post-crisis reforms:**
- Dodd-Frank required SEC oversight of Nationally Recognized Statistical Rating Organizations (NRSROs)
- Removal of regulatory reliance on ratings (partially implemented)
- Enhanced disclosure of rating methodologies and performance tracking
- Multiple rating agencies per deal became standard practice

### 6. Credit Enhancement

Credit enhancement (CE) is the structural mechanism that protects senior bondholders from losses. Key forms include:

- **Subordination**: Junior tranches absorb losses before senior tranches.
- **Over-collateralization (OC)**: Collateral balance exceeds bond balance.
- **Excess spread**: Difference between collateral coupon and bond coupon provides a first line of defense.
- **Reserve funds**: Cash set aside at closing to cover shortfalls.
- **Bond insurance** (less common post-crisis): Third-party guarantee on specific tranches.

*(Covered in detail in [Credit Enhancement](./18-credit-enhancement.md))*

### 7. Post-Crisis Market Developments

#### Credit Risk Transfer (CRT)

- **Purpose**: GSEs transfer mortgage credit risk to private capital markets.
- **Fannie Mae**: Connecticut Avenue Securities (CAS) program.
- **Freddie Mac**: Structured Agency Credit Risk (STACR) program.
- **Structure**: Bonds reference a pool of agency-guaranteed mortgages; investors bear credit losses.
- **Significance**: Largest single category of non-agency issuance (~$30–50 billion annually).

*(Covered in detail in [Credit Risk Transfer](./20-credit-risk-transfer.md))*

#### RPL/NPL Securitization

- **GSE sales**: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac sold billions in re-performing and non-performing loan pools to reduce their retained portfolios.
- **Private securitization**: Buyers securitized these pools, creating a new asset class.
- **Data engineering challenges**: Complex loan histories, modification waterfalls, servicer advances, and loss timeline modeling.

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Prime Jumbo Deal — JPMMT 2025-1

A typical post-crisis prime jumbo deal:
- **Issuer**: JP Morgan Mortgage Trust
- **Collateral**: 500 prime jumbo loans, weighted average FICO 770, weighted average LTV 68%, full documentation
- **Structure**: Senior/sub with 5.50% initial credit enhancement on the AAA tranche
- **Ratings**: AAA/AA/A/BBB from two or more agencies
- **Spread**: AAA tranche priced at SOFR + 75 bps

### Example 2: Non-QM Deal — VERUS 2025-3

- **Issuer**: Verus Securitization Trust
- **Collateral**: 1,200 non-QM loans (bank statement, DSCR, asset depletion), weighted average FICO 735, weighted average LTV 72%
- **Structure**: Senior/sub with 30.00% initial credit enhancement on AAA
- **Key risk**: Non-standard documentation requires higher CE levels; investor property concentration adds risk
- **Data fields**: Bank statement months, DSCR ratio, asset depletion methodology

### Example 3: Pre-Crisis Subprime — HEAT 2006-5

- **Issuer**: Home Equity Asset Trust (HSBC)
- **Collateral**: 5,000 subprime loans, weighted average FICO 620, weighted average CLTV 85%, high stated-income percentage
- **Structure**: Six-level capital structure; AAA tranche had only 18% CE
- **Outcome**: Experienced 45%+ cumulative losses; all tranches below AAA wiped out; AAA tranches suffered principal losses
- **Lesson**: Inadequate stress testing, flawed correlation assumptions, and origination fraud led to catastrophic losses

## Common Interview Questions & Answers

### Q1: What is the difference between agency and non-agency MBS?

**Answer**: Agency MBS are guaranteed by Ginnie Mae (explicitly by the US government) or by GSEs (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) with an implicit/conservatorship guarantee. Investors bear prepayment risk but essentially no credit risk. Non-agency MBS have no government guarantee — investors bear both prepayment and credit risk. Non-agency deals require credit enhancement (subordination, OC, excess spread) to protect senior tranches. Non-agency collateral includes loans that don't meet agency eligibility criteria: jumbo balances, non-standard documentation, lower credit scores, or non-QM features.

### Q2: Why did the non-agency market collapse during the financial crisis, and what structural changes were made post-crisis?

**Answer**: The collapse was driven by multiple factors: (1) aggressive underwriting — stated income, high CLTV, teaser rates with payment shock; (2) national home price declines eliminated the "refinance escape valve" borrowers relied on; (3) rating agency models did not stress for correlated nationwide declines; (4) misaligned incentives in the originate-to-distribute model; (5) inadequate credit enhancement levels. Post-crisis reforms include: Dodd-Frank ATR/QM rules, 5% risk retention requirement (Reg RR, though QM-exempt), enhanced Reg AB II disclosure, mandatory third-party due diligence, loan-level data transparency, stronger representations and warranties with independent breach reviewers, and significantly higher CE levels.

### Q3: What is a non-QM loan and why is this sector growing?

**Answer**: A non-QM loan fails to meet the CFPB's Qualified Mortgage definition but still complies with the Ability-to-Repay rule. Common reasons include: reliance on bank statements rather than tax returns (self-employed borrowers), debt service coverage ratio-based underwriting (investor properties), interest-only payment features, or asset depletion qualification. The sector is growing because it serves creditworthy borrowers whose income or property use doesn't fit into the traditional documentation box. Self-employed borrowers, real estate investors, and foreign nationals are significant populations underserved by agency programs. As a data engineer, non-QM securitizations require tracking additional non-standard data fields like months of bank statements, DSCR calculations, and asset depletion schedules.

### Q4: How do rating agencies evaluate non-agency MBS?

**Answer**: Rating agencies use proprietary models to estimate expected losses under various stress scenarios. The process involves: (1) loan-level analysis of credit attributes (FICO, LTV, DTI, documentation type, occupancy, property type, geography); (2) application of base-case and stressed default/severity assumptions; (3) cash flow modeling through the deal waterfall to determine if each tranche can withstand losses consistent with its rating level (e.g., AAA must survive a Great Depression-level scenario); (4) qualitative assessment of originator, servicer, and structural features. Post-crisis, agencies publish their criteria publicly and must demonstrate ongoing surveillance. From a data engineering perspective, we need to map our loan data to each agency's required input fields and run scenario analyses.

### Q5: What are the key data engineering challenges specific to non-agency MBS?

**Answer**: Key challenges include: (1) **Heterogeneous data**: Unlike agency MBS with standardized data formats, each non-agency issuer may have different tape layouts, field definitions, and delivery formats; (2) **Loan-level disclosure**: Reg AB II requires extensive loan-level data (FICO, LTV, DTI, occupancy, property type, geography, modification history) — building pipelines to ingest and normalize this data across issuers is complex; (3) **Historical data**: Legacy deals from pre-crisis may have incomplete or inconsistent data; (4) **Performance tracking**: Monthly remittance reports require parsing servicer reports with varying formats; (5) **RPL/NPL complexity**: Re-performing loans have modification histories, previous delinquency timelines, and servicer advance recoverability that require specialized data models; (6) **CRT reference pools**: Tracking credit events against GSE reference pools requires integration with agency disclosure data.

## Tips for Interview Success

1. **Know the timeline**: Be prepared to discuss the pre-crisis boom, the crisis itself, and the post-crisis revival as a coherent narrative. Interviewers want to see that you understand why things failed and what changed.

2. **Understand the regulatory landscape**: Dodd-Frank, ATR/QM, risk retention, and Reg AB II are foundational knowledge. Know how these regulations reshaped issuance and data requirements.

3. **Differentiate collateral types**: Be able to clearly explain the differences between prime jumbo, alt-A, subprime, non-QM, RPL, and NPL — their borrower profiles, risk characteristics, and data requirements.

4. **Connect to data engineering**: Always tie your answers back to data — what fields matter, how data flows through the securitization lifecycle, and what challenges arise in building pipelines for non-agency products.

5. **Quantify when possible**: Citing approximate issuance volumes, CE levels, loss rates, and spread levels demonstrates depth of knowledge and market awareness.

6. **Be honest about legacy vs. current market**: The pre-crisis and post-crisis non-agency markets are fundamentally different products. Show you understand the evolution.
